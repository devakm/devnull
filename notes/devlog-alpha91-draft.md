# alpha91 devlog draft (for review)

## Intro

alpha91 is the biggest OOORF release yet, and it landed on two fronts at once. On the items side, fourteen brand-new armor sets arrived — each one a long-standing OOO armor family finally given its own dedicated look instead of borrowing a vanilla appearance. On the world side, the bundled dungeons got the four hardest problems in the project solved and shipped: water that you can actually swim in, campfires whose flames render correctly, surfaces that no longer flicker, and ghost objects that no longer haunt the modded caves. This devlog is the making-of story behind both — the long debugging stretches, the lucky discoveries, and the moments where a "finished" fix turned out to be quietly broken.

## Arc cards (6)

- **Taming water** | `a91-water` | The dungeons had no water of their own, so the team learned to transplant a working water body out of a clean vanilla cave and graft it into place — then spent days fighting surfaces that clipped short and swim volumes that triggered nowhere.
- **Fire: 15 tries to a bonfire** | `a91-fire` | A single campfire took fifteen build-and-test cycles and five distinct structural bugs to render its sparks, flames, light, and smoke — an eight-day descent into the guts of Unreal's particle-system format.
- **Flicker & ghost suppression** | `a91-flicker` | Modded dungeons stacked two copies of every wall, so surfaces strobed as the engine couldn't decide which to draw; the fix was to switch off the redundant twin — and then re-seat every chest left floating in mid-air.
- **Icons & GND meshes** | `a91-icons` | Recoloring inventory icons in their compressed form caused a glow-bleed, then a crash-to-desktop that masqueraded as a 3D-mesh bug for a full day before a binary search pinned the real culprit.
- **The recolor pipeline (fur is a baked color)** | `a91-recolor` | New armor sets aren't modeled, they're re-dyed — and the wave's best discovery was that fur color isn't a texture at all but two 16-byte color values baked into the material, making a dreaded job a clean surgical edit.
- **The 14-set armor wave** | `a91-armor` | Fourteen sets shipped in five weeks off one shared recolor pipeline, each with its own drama — from ShadowMail's hand-painted chainmail mask to Aureus's gold unicorn emblem to a naming trap that recolored the wrong leather entirely.

## Chronological spine (oldest -> newest within alpha91)

- `2026-04-04 | a91-flicker | First proof that the flicker fix works | Oblivion Remastered is really two games stacked together: a modern Unreal Engine 5 world re-baked from the original, sitting on top of the old Oblivion data that still drives the logic underneath. The OOO mod injects its own copy of dungeon architecture back into that world, so in modded caves many walls and pillars existed twice — once as the gorgeous UE5-baked geometry, once as the mod's older duplicate in the exact same spot. When two surfaces fight for the identical pixel, the graphics card alternates between them frame by frame, and that shimmer is the flicker players reported. On this day the team proved the cure on a single duplicated pedestal in a test dungeon: don't delete anything, just flip an "initially disabled" flag on the mod's twin so it never spawns. The beautiful UE5 original stays, along with its collision and navigation, and the flicker vanishes.`
- `2026-04-16 | - | Drowned Hopes Cave becomes the model cave | The first dungeon to get water and ghost-suppression working together end-to-end, used afterward as the reference recipe. Its first zone was confirmed in-game this day.`
- `2026-04-17 | - | Drowned Hopes Cave, second zone | The second zone's water confirmed in-game — a "z-range only" case that didn't need the full surface rebuild.`
- `2026-05-09 | - | The armor wave kicks off | The team sits down and maps out all of OOO's armor families — roughly sixty-eight sets — and lays the groundwork for building new looks by recoloring existing ones rather than modeling from scratch.`
- `2026-05-10 | - | Deep Cover Cave, first zone | The first cave with no vanilla water of its own to lean on; water transplanted from a donor cave and confirmed in-game, establishing the offset rule used for every later water job.`
- `2026-05-11 | - | Deep Cover Cave, second zone | Second zone confirmed; an "invisible obstacle" bug, where a suppressed container left behind ghost collision you could walk into, was fixed here.`
- `2026-05-15 | - | The unify campaign begins — Arondar and Narind | From mid-May the team ran the "unify" campaign: a long series of numbered in-game retests that walked each modded dungeon end-to-end, confirming flicker was gone, ghosts were suppressed, and chests rested correctly. Arondar (three zones) and Narind (two zones) were confirmed this day. Narind also surfaced a nasty historical regression — a whole batch of ghost-suppression entries had been silently deleted months earlier under an unrelated change — which was recovered not by hand-patching but by teaching the suppression tool new rules so the entries regenerate themselves. This is also where the project's two opposite cleanup problems became clear. "TES4 suppression" drops the mod's redundant old copy of something the remaster already baked in (the flicker fix). The "Begone" tool does the reverse: it removes a UE5-baked object — a stray door, torch, or cobweb — that has no counterpart in the mod's layout and shows up as an orphaned ghost. Same goal, one object per spot; you just delete from whichever layer holds the unwanted twin.`
- `2026-05-16 | - | Cehani validated, and Begone changes how it works | Both Cehani zones confirmed in-game. The headline this day was the "Begone migration." Begone had run as a live mod that scanned each zone as you entered and hid ghost actors on the fly — but that scanning was fragile, kept references to engine objects that went stale during zone transitions, and hard-crashed the game when it touched one. The cure was to stop hiding ghosts at runtime and instead edit the cooked map files directly before the game ever loads them, so the engine simply skips each ghost at load. No live scanning, no stale references, no crash. The live mod was retired to a debug-only logging role and won't ship in the final package. Cehani also opened a volumetric-fog mystery — an OOO-injected fog effect that drew an abrupt flat-plane edge despite being byte-identical to vanilla.`
- `2026-05-17 | - | Yeleri and Barastas validated | Yeleri (three zones) and Barastas (two zones) confirmed. Yeleri's altar-chests kicked off the placement framework that would soon expose a load-bearing measurement bug.`
- `2026-05-18 | - | Dostares, and the floating-chest problem | Dostares (two zones) confirmed on retest. This stretch crystallized the most unglamorous side effect of the whole suppression effort: when you switch off a duplicated platform, sarcophagus, or table, anything resting on it suddenly has nothing beneath it and hangs in mid-air. A treasure chest that sat on a tomb lid now floats a foot above where the lid used to be. The fix is "position overrides" — manually re-seating each orphaned chest, bedroll, and furniture piece onto the UE5-baked surface that's still there. It sounds trivial but became painstaking, because the UE5 copy of a sarcophagus often sits at a slightly different height than the old one did, and the offset wasn't uniform — even two identical sarcophagi in the same room could rest at different heights. A scale-conversion bug discovered here had been quietly throwing off every height calculation.`
- `2026-05-19 | - | Rosulas validated | Both Rosulas zones confirmed. The durable lesson locked in here: a chest's correct resting height is measured against the UE5-baked surface beneath it, and the mod author's own original placement is the strongest clue to where the chest belongs.`
- `2026-05-20 | - | Four dungeons in one day | Varastal, Lipsand Tarn 03, Thundering Steps Cave (three zones), and Broken Tooth Cave (two zones) all confirmed — several on a single clean walkthrough, thanks to a new tool that derives chest heights automatically instead of by hand.`
- `2026-05-22 | - | Grave Ground Cave validated, with a rock in the doorway | Grave Ground Cave confirmed, including a spun-off side fix: the cave entrance was blocked by a UE5-baked rock that neither suppression method could touch, because it turned out to be a foliage instance rather than a normal actor. That one needed a separate workflow that shifts the rock in the cooked foliage data — and produced the new GraveGroundExterior pak, the bundle's first non-dungeon map.`
- `2026-05-22 | - | The hard weeks of water begin — Blood Clot Cave | Blood Clot Cave is an OOO-added, Argonian-themed dungeon, and getting it flooded turned out to require four independent systems to all agree at once: the water surface you see from above, the underwater haze you see while submerged, the swim volume that lets you float and paddle, and the breath/drowning meter. Each is driven by different engine data and each can fail silently on its own. The cave had no usable water of its own, so the team borrowed one — a "donor cell." Think of it as organ transplantation for game levels: copy the entire working water apparatus out of a clean vanilla cave (a simple rectangular pool was chosen over bigger but messier candidates) and graft it into Blood Clot Cave, then spend most of your effort getting it to not reject the graft.`
- `2026-05-23 | - | "Swims nowhere" solved | For two sessions every diagnostic insisted the swim volume existed and sat in exactly the right place — yet the player never triggered a swim stroke. The cause was almost absurd: a copied water brush carries a hidden internal pointer to its collision body, stored as a raw slot number. In the large donor cave that number happened to land on a valid object; in the smaller Blood Clot Cave the same number landed on an unrelated decal, so the engine couldn't build the swim collision and quietly gave up. Re-pointing the brush at a valid collision body in the target fixed it. The team also measured a water-height "golden rule" against roughly ninety real vanilla cells, correcting a small scale error that had quietly haunted weeks of earlier work.`
- `2026-05-26 | a91-water | Blood Clot Cave zone 1 verified | The last and hardest piece of zone 1 was the visible water surface, which kept clipping off at a clean vertical line partway down the cave. Four straight builds extending meshes, bounds, splines, and zone boxes each did absolutely nothing, and after forty-plus iterations the team had even concluded the surface was permanently baked at cook time and unfixable. The real answer was buried in the actual UE5 engine source: the visible lake surface is shaped by a separate, high-precision copy of the collision hull's vertices — a different copy from the one they'd been editing all along. Extend that copy, and the water covered the whole cave. Zone 1 went fully verified in-game this day, ghost-fire suppression and all.`
- `2026-05-27 | - | Blood Clot Cave zone 2 verified | You'd hope zone 1 made the rest free; it didn't, and that disappointment is part of the story. Zone 2 needed the water lifted to a new height and pushed into a previously-dry upper hall, and it exposed that "the surface" is gated by a whole chain of elements where the smallest one clips everything — including an outermost tile grid nobody had realized was a separate gate. Zone 2 burned roughly nine more test cycles rediscovering levers, which finally pushed the team to build an offline quality check that catches a half-finished water footprint before the game is ever launched.`
- `2026-06-01 | a91-fire | Fire: fifteen tries to a bonfire | Blood Clot Cave's third zone needed something water never did: two working campfires. And here the eight-day gap since zone 1's water makes sense — none of it was more water work, it was one brutal sub-problem. A water volume belongs to an always-loaded engine module, but a campfire is a complex blueprint with a particle system, and cloning one out of a different vanilla package than the cave it's going into tripped a cascade of at least seven distinct low-level bugs. The villain was a "byte-scan" that blindly rewrites any number that looks like an internal reference — and a particle system is full of innocent numbers (parameter values, name indices, offset fields) that coincidentally look like references and got corrupted. Each fix peeled back a layer: a header bit-layout that had been wrong since the very first fire build, parameter names that didn't survive the packaging tool's pruning, crashes from over-restoring data, and finally one specific offset value (the number 12, which happened to equal an internal index) being scrambled and killing the flame spawn rate. Along the way the team found that the vanilla bonfire effect itself has a bug — its flames overflow the woodpile mesh — and compensated by shrinking the scale. On the fifteenth iteration it all lit: sparks, fire, light, and smoke, all rendering correctly. The cross-package injection method is now reusable for any future fire job.`
- `2026-06-02 | - | ElvenNight — the set that proved recoloring was possible | ElvenNight was the first armor set built by the new recolor approach, and it had to overturn an old assumption that you needed a special "shell" to ship a recolored texture. The discovery: vanilla textures ship fine on their own if you preserve their full structure and exact byte size — recolor the top image, regenerate the smaller zoom levels, and rename the texture in its own folder. The whole armor wave rests on this idea of re-dyeing existing armor rather than modeling new geometry. The game already ships beautifully made meshes and textures; the pipeline takes a vanilla set and produces a recolored copy the game treats as a brand-new item. A naive hue-shift looks dead and plasticky, so the central rule emerged early: recolor by multiplying the existing light-to-dark variation up or down, never by flattening every pixel to one target value — that variation is what makes a material read as itself. ElvenNight also set up the original metal/cloth/leather region recipe that every later set built on. [THUMB: ElvenNight]`
- `2026-06-03 | - | The pipeline gets quality gates | The recolor pipeline grows automated checks: one verifies every shipped texture is structurally valid, one verifies that renaming a cloned asset didn't break an internal reference, and later ones confirm a recolor's region assignment matches the vanilla layout. A recurring theme of the whole project: the measurement someone asked for often turned out to be the diagnostic that proved a "finished" fix was actually wrong. Build the gate, and the truth follows. This same session scoped the Elven follow-on sets — ElvenSky and ElvenEldar — as pure config-and-tuning jobs to be built on top of the now-generic pipeline.`
- `2026-06-04 | - | Eboron — the first Mithril variant | Eboron is where the four enduring metal-recolor lessons were learned in-game: bright rings on the mail were gold rivets mis-classed as plate (filtered out by area); chainmail has to be the default surface or its brightest highlights stay vanilla white; "dark metal" comes from darkening the mid-tones while keeping highlights, not crushing everything to black; and a brightness cap lets trim shine silver while rivets stay dark at the same time. Built and confirmed in-game this day. [THUMB: Eboron]`
- `2026-06-04 | - | ShadowMail — the most-iterated set in the wave | ShadowMail, the second Mithril variant, was first built this day and then fought back harder than anything else over the following days. It had to look distinct from Eboron, so it got a cool blue-steel hue with deliberate saturation floors (too low and the blue simply vanishes into grey). Its leather went through a muddy "poop red" and a washed-out "latte" before landing on dark mahogany. And it eventually grew into a full four-region model — steel-blue trim, very dark blue plate body, black chainmail, and faded-black skirt leather — with the chainmail hand-painted into a mask because no texture statistic could separate it from the body. ShadowMail is essentially where every region technique in the wave was invented under fire. [THUMB: ShadowMail]`
- `2026-06-05 | - | Leather techniques — continuity zones and grain | Refinement work on the leather sets produced two reusable tricks. Some vanilla textures have baked-in defects — on one cuirass the top strip of a leather back panel is genuinely blurry in the original art. After about twenty-five iterations trying to synthesize grain to fix it, the breakthrough was to stop synthesizing and search the rest of the texture sheet for a clean, undamaged copy of the same panel hiding elsewhere, then paste that pristine copy over the defect. The second trick: transplant fine leather grain from a sharp region into a blurred one, taking only the fine detail while keeping the original's color, so it adds realism without washing out the dye.`
- `2026-06-07 | - | Aureus, and the boots that show their soles | Aureus, the third Mithril variant, was built this day (silver body with copper chainmail), with a custom gold rampant-unicorn emblem on its skirt panels that went through eleven revisions. This was also the day recolored "ground meshes" — the 3D model you see when an item is dropped on the floor or spun in the preview — were first wired into the pipeline, and they brought a head-scratcher: dropped boots lay on their backs showing their soles. The mesh was byte-for-byte perfect with no rotation baked in, so where did the wrong angle come from? The game stores each item's display angle in a separate lookup table keyed by the mesh's name. Rename the mesh to its recolored version, and the lookup finds nothing and falls back to a default rotation — boots flop over. The deliberate call: show players the real recolored art even at the wrong angle for now, and file the rotation as a post-launch fix. [THUMB: Aureus]`
- `2026-06-08 | - | Aureus and the tribal pattern that wouldn't die | The unicorn skirt cleared up, but the front tabard's tribal engraving refused to disappear no matter what was done to its texture. After elimination the cause was proven to be in no texture at all: the tribal relief is baked into the mesh's own vertex normals and geometry, and a flat texture can't cancel a shape that's modeled in. The honest outcome — lean the unicorn on top of it rather than fight it — became a reusable diagnostic: ask whether a relief lives in the texture or the mesh before spending a session chasing it in the wrong place.`
- `2026-06-09 | - | ElvenSky and ElvenEldar take shape | The two remaining Elven tiers — ElvenSky and ElvenEldar — come together as the planned payoff of the now-generic recolor pipeline, built as config-and-tuning jobs rather than from scratch. Neither set has a dedicated build-day handoff; by the completeness pass a few days later all three Elven sets are referenced as a finished, gate-passing trio, so this is the best-supported window for their completion. [THUMB: ElvenSky] [THUMB: ElvenEldar]`
- `2026-06-10 | a91-recolor | The fur sets — discovering fur is a baked color | When the team reached the Fur sets, the trim looked like a nightmare: fur is rendered with a special hair-card shader, and common sense said recoloring it meant decoding some odd packed texture, repainting it, and praying it didn't corrupt. Reconnaissance found something far better — the fur's color isn't stored in a texture at all. It lives as two values inside the material itself, a root color and a tip color (giving the natural base-to-tip gradient), stored as plain 16-byte color values right in the material's parameter block. The format was verified against Unreal Engine 5.3's own source, and the lucky part was that those two color overrides already existed in the vanilla material, set to a brown gradient. That turned a dreaded job into a clean surgical edit: find the two 16-byte slots by pattern and overwrite them in place — no repainting, no re-encoding, none of the file-rewriting where corruption usually creeps in. ArcticFur became cool snow-white by writing two new colors; WornFur simply left the vanilla brown untouched. One gotcha rode along: the fur helmet's body texture was the only one in a different compression format, which made its real dimensions look garbled until the format was confirmed — a reminder to always check a texture's true format before trusting its size. Both fur sets were built green this day. [THUMB: ArcticFur] [THUMB: WornFur]`
- `2026-06-10 | - | GoldenBronze closes the Mithril tier | The last Mithril variant, built and confirmed in-game and merged this day. It reinforced the multiply-don't-flatten rule one final time: its engraved helmet was losing its line-work to a hard brightness cap, and switching to a value-preserving multiply "added the black channel back." [THUMB: GoldenBronze]`
- `2026-06-11 | - | ArcticFur and WornFur approved in-game | The two fur sets, built the day before, were tuned and approved in a live in-game test this day.`
- `2026-06-11 | - | WornLeather and the "Thief/Pit" naming trap | WornLeather's first build came out wrong in-game — the body showed vanilla — thanks to a genuinely confusing piece of original game data. The armor players call "Leather" is actually built from a "Thief"-codenamed asset graph, while the assets literally named "Leather" are an unused arena-gladiator set vanilla never shipped. The first build recolored the wrong chain. The fix meant tracing the entire display chain from item to blueprint to mesh to material to texture, then retargeting onto the real body. Out of this came reusable machinery for mix-and-match sets and a tuning lesson: in-game lighting runs noticeably brighter than the flat preview, so previews should be biased darker. Shipped and verified this day. [THUMB: WornLeather]`
- `2026-06-11 | - | BloodLeather and GrayFox ship | Both built cleanly off the WornLeather groundwork and verified in-game this day — BloodLeather in a deep-red dye, GrayFox a grey body with shadowed straps (and a stray bracer that is genuinely Leather-named, handled by the new mix-and-match machinery). [THUMB: BloodLeather] [THUMB: GrayFox]`
- `2026-06-12 | a91-armor | The 14-set wave completes — glass rename and recolor completeness | The wave's final pass renamed the two oldest sets from their developer codenames to their player-facing names: BGlass became BlueGlass and RGlass became Drakefired (which also picked up new red icons, since its old ones had been copied from the blue set). The rename taught a sharp process lesson — it was declared "done" on passing builds, but the override file that binds the game's items to the new asset paths still pointed at the old names, so "done" now means enumerating the entire deploy set, not just the build. With BlueGlass and Drakefired re-released, all fourteen sets — ArcticFur, Aureus, BloodLeather, BlueGlass, Drakefired, Eboron, ElvenEldar, ElvenNight, ElvenSky, GoldenBronze, GrayFox, ShadowMail, WornFur, and WornLeather — were complete. [THUMB: BlueGlass] [THUMB: Drakefired]`
- `2026-06-12 | - | Icons & GND meshes — the glow bleed | Every item has a little inventory picture, and for this mod those are recolored in their compressed form (a format called DXT5 that keeps a transparency channel for clean cut-out edges). An in-game test this day showed the recoloring bleeding past the item's silhouette: the game draws a soft glow halo around each icon, and the recoloring was tinting the glow's bright pixels as if they were metal. A second symptom rode along — stray black spots, caused deep in the compression math when two reference colors in a block get set to the same value. The instinct was to surgically rewrite each icon's transparency data to erase the glow. It looked right in preview. It was a trap.`
- `2026-06-13 | a91-icons | The crash that pretended to be a mesh bug | The next session opened brutally: opening the inventory crashed the game to desktop, and many icons rendered as blank parchment or broken purple squares. Because the test character carried every item at once, there was no way to tell whether the icons, the dropped-item preview meshes, or the sheer weight of the bag was the trigger — and early suspicion fell hard on the 3D meshes, burning real effort comparing them byte by byte. The breakthrough came from a binary search: build a stripped-down save with a single armor set and run the icons through the game's own parser. The smoking gun appeared — the icons still claimed the right format, but their stored width and height were garbage, nonsensical numbers in the billions. The game can't build an image from impossible dimensions, so it failed the texture and crashed. The root cause traced straight back to the previous day's "fix": editing the transparency portion of a compressed icon corrupted the repacking bookkeeping, but only for icons storing multiple zoom levels — which is exactly why the bug hid, since the one always-fine icon was the lone single-level one. The retreat was clean: never touch an icon's transparency, only its color, and leave the glow untinted-but-present rather than risk deleting it. A new quality gate now runs the game's own parser over every shipped icon and fails the build on any impossible size. Two side bugs — a fur set reverted to brown and a truncated package — were swept up the same day, and everything was confirmed in-game.`

## Fact-check notes

- **Dungeon validation count (15 of 17).** The phase9-unify-tracker lists 17 distinct OOO dungeons. Fifteen have a full in-game validation pass; the two exceptions are **Gloom Way Cave** and **Red Gill Cave**, which carry only a heuristic "probably walked" suppression status (substantial Begone entry counts) and remain untested on the other phases. This matches the "15 of 17 dungeons validated" framing. The spine covers all 17 dungeons by validation date; Gloom Way and Red Gill are intentionally not given milestone bullets because they were not freshly validated within the alpha91 window — flag for the reviewer if a dungeon-by-dungeon completeness list is wanted in the published page.
- **"7 dungeon paks rebuilt" vs. "17 dungeons."** These are different sets and both correct. The release ships **7 rebuilt dungeon paks (14 zones)** — the subset whose cooked maps actually changed since alpha90 (Blood Clot, Broken Tooth, Deep Cover, Grave Ground, Lipsand Tarn 03, Rosulas, Thundering Steps). The "17 dungeons / 15 validated" figure is the project-wide validation tally across all OOO dungeons, most of which were already shipped in earlier releases. The spine keeps both framings distinct.
- **All key figures verified against the release docs.** 14 new armor sets, 2 removed (BGlass/RGlass), ~307 SyncMap rebindings, +254 disabled REFRs, +17 position overrides, +92 Begone entries, 7 paks / 14 zones — all confirmed to match both `alpha91.md` and this repo's `changelog.html` / `overview.html`. No discrepancies found between the handoffs and the release docs on these numbers.
- **Fire "15 iterations."** The fire handoff's own iteration log runs T3-v5 through T3-v15, i.e. the successful build was the fifteenth tagged iteration; it cites five distinct structural bugs (plus extra fixes/compensations). The arc card and spine use "fifteen tries" and "five structural bugs," consistent with the source. The handoff occasionally describes "at least seven" low-level fixes counting compensations — the prose uses "five structural bugs" to match the release-facing framing while noting the larger cascade.
- **ElvenSky / ElvenEldar build dates are inferred, not documented.** No ItemClone handoff records a dedicated build day for these two sets. They were scoped on 2026-06-03 as follow-on Elven tiers and appear complete in the 2026-06-12 completeness pass; the spine places them at **2026-06-09** as a best-supported midpoint. Flag for the reviewer: if an exact date matters for the published page, it would need to come from git history rather than the handoffs.
- **ShadowMail and Aureus span multiple days.** Both were first built on their "build day" (06-04 and 06-07) but polished across later sessions (ShadowMail through 06-06; Aureus's tribal-mesh resolution on 06-08). The spine anchors each set's primary milestone on its build day and gives the later drama its own bullet, so every set is represented without double-counting.
- **No raw identifiers in the prose.** All EDIDs, FormID hex, iteration tags (e.g. BCCz3-T3-v15), and commit hashes from the sources have been translated out, per the brief.
